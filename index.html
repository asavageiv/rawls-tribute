Introduction

John Rawls is my favorite political philosopher. He gave me tools to reason my way through the thicket of political ideas and a sense of which direction justice lies. I think his ideas should be common knowledge, and reasons for our political beliefs should be actively discussed. As such, for my tribute to Rawls, I present his principles of justice and an outline of their reasoning.

The Principles of Justice as Fairness

If you know nothing else from Rawls, know this. The principles of justice that would be chosen for a society that is "a fair system of cooperation between free and equal citizens from one generation to the next" are as follows:

  1. First Principle: Each person has the same indefeasible claim to a fully adequate scheme of equal basic liberties, which scheme is compatible with the same scheme of liberties for all.
  2. Second Principle: Social and economic inequalities are to satisfy two conditions:
    1. They are to be attached to offices and positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity.
    2. They are to be to the greatest benefit of the least-advantaged members of society (the difference principle).

They are prioritized such that the first principle is to be satisfied before the second, and fair equality of opportunity is to be satisfied before the difference principle. Furthermore, these principles only apply to the basic institutions of society. For example, they can be used to show that property-owning democracy and democratic socialism are compatible with the principles of justice, but laissez-faire capitalism, welfare-state capitalism, and socialist command economies are not. They they can not be used to compare two actual people, see that one is worse off and then say that means they deserve to get something from someone who is better off.

If you are interested in why these are the principles of justice, read on.

Society as a Fair System of Cooperation

"The correct regulative principle for anything depends on the nature of that thing."
A Theory of Justice

Rawls attacks the problem of justice and society at its roots by starting with its basic structure. By basic structure we mean things like the laws laid down in the constitution and the economic system of society. If these things are unjust then we have no hope of a just society because all other activity takes place within the context of these structures. In order to determine whether some basic structure is just or not we must have principles to guide us, these will be the principles of justice.

To come up with reasonable principles, we have to be clear about what we're talking about. What do we mean by "society". Rawls defines society as, "a fair system of cooperation among its members from one generation to the next". This definition is why Rawls's conception of justice is called "justice as fairness". It has its roots in the ideas of social contracts, that the legitimacy of government derives from the consent of the governed1, and the fact of reasonable plurality in society (that is, given enough people, differing experience will create reasonable diagreement). Note that society could be conceived of differently. For example, monarchies were often thought to derive their legitimacy from divine right. Rawls's definition is broader and more vague so it might bring more of us on board. It relies on the idea of fairness, which is widely considered important when dealing with questions of justice.

This brings up the question of what we mean by "fair". Rawls answers this by changing the question to, “What terms of cooperation would free and equal citizens agree to under fair conditions?”. This makes the problem more tractable by allowing us to define fair conditions and then considering what would be agreed to under those conditions.

The Original Position and the Veil of Ignorance

"In justice as fairness, men agree to share one another's fate."
A Theory of Justice

To have fair conditions, personal biases and historical biases must be removed, so Rawls asks us to imagine ourselves in an original position behind a veil of ignorance. In the original position a group of representatives must unanimously decide on the principles of justice for society. The veil of ignorance prevents them from knowing specifics about themselves or society that would allow them to bias those principles in their own favor, but they know general things about humanity. For example, they know everyone wants to pursue their own idea of the good, and to have more of primary goods like rights and liberties, freedom of movement and choice of occupation, income and wealth, but they do not know their race, religion, wealth, nationality, or when they would enter into society. This is sort of like telling the person cutting a cake that they will not get to decide who gets which slice.

The complete reasoning from the original position to the principles of justice above is quite long, so I will only outline it intuitively here. For the first principle, one can imagine that if they didn't know who they'd be in society, they'd want to protect themselves from becoming slaves or otherwise being disenfranchised, so they would want basic rights. They could only get everyone to agree to this if everyone gets the same set of rights.

For the second principle, one can imagine that they'd want to protect themselves from bad luck. If you're to be born poor, or slightly stupid, or in the wrong place without a lot of natural resources, you don't want your life to be shitty. But you don't want perfect equality either. If some inequality creates greater wealth for everyone, even the least well off, then it is still desirable. With this in mind one might agree to the second principle, which prevents gaining wealth at the expense of the least advantaged, but still encourages talent, hard work, and value production for society through some admissable inequality.

Conclusion

This is just a basic outline from a conception of society to some basic principles and the logic can be taken much further. If you are interested in more details of how these principles are derived or more of Rawls's ideas, I strongly recommend the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy Entry on John Rawls. Reading that will probably be the smartest thing you do all year.
